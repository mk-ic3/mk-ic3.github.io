Undergraduate repair — parallel sprint
Weeks 1–8 · Run alongside Phase 1, not before it
The key insight: don't finish UG before starting plasma. Run repair in parallel — identify gaps as plasma topics demand them, then patch precisely. A 100% UG review before starting is a trap that costs a semester.
Electromagnetism — highest priority
Griffiths Ch 7–10 + Jackson Ch 6–8
Master the full differential form of Maxwell's equations. Focus: Faraday's law in integral vs. differential form, displacement current, energy in fields (Poynting vector), plane wave solutions, and boundary conditions. The plasma Ohm's law is just a modified Ampere's law — you cannot proceed without this.
Griffiths (intro) Jackson (advanced) Derive Maxwell's eqs from scratch weekly Gap: Faraday in differential form
Classical mechanics — high priority
Goldstein Ch 1–3 · Lagrangian, Hamiltonian, canonical momentum
Plasma: the canonical momentum p = mv + qA appears everywhere in single-particle theory and in the derivation of adiabatic invariants. Hamiltonian mechanics underpins the Vlasov equation (Liouville's theorem). You need: action-angle variables, Poisson brackets, and the meaning of phase-space volume preservation.
Goldstein Solve orbit problems with Lagrangian Gap: canonical momentum with B field
Statistical mechanics — medium priority
Reif or Kittel · Phase space, distributions, ergodicity
The distribution function f(x,v,t) is the core object of kinetic plasma theory. You need: Maxwell-Boltzmann derivation (why it minimises free energy), Boltzmann's H-theorem, and the concept of ergodicity. Bonus: entropy production and irreversibility set the stage for understanding collisions and Landau damping.
Reif (accessible) Derive Maxwell-Boltzmann from scratch
Mathematical methods — ongoing
Arfken or Boas · PDEs, Fourier, complex analysis, tensors
Fourier transform in 3D is used for dispersion relations (substitute e^{ikx−iωt} into linearised plasma equations → algebraic system). Contour integration in complex ω-plane gives Landau damping. Tensor index notation is unavoidable in MHD. Don't study this in isolation — let plasma problems drive which chapter you need next.
Arfken Derive dispersion relations from scratch Mathematica for contour integrals
Self-check milestones — tick when you can do it with book closed
Write all 4 Maxwell equations in differential form from memory
Derive the Lorentz force law from the Lagrangian L = (1/2)mv² − q(φ − v·A)
State Liouville's theorem and explain what it implies for f(x,v,t)
Derive the Fourier representation of a plane wave and substitute into ∇²φ = 0
Compute the Poynting vector for a plane electromagnetic wave

Core plasma physics
Months 2–6 · Primary textbook phase
Primary text: Kulsrud — Plasma Physics for Astrophysics (the best single book for your goals). Supplement with Boyd & Sanderson for kinetic theory and Chen for intuition.
Month 2 · Weeks 5–8
Single-particle motion — the vocabulary
Gyromotion (derive cyclotron frequency ω_c = qB/m and Larmor radius r_L = mv⊥/qB). All four guiding centre drifts (E×B, grad-B, curvature, polarisation). Three adiabatic invariants (μ, J, Φ) — each conserved on a different timescale. Magnetic mirrors and the loss cone angle θ_loss = arcsin(√B_min/B_max). These are the building blocks everything else rests on.
Kulsrud Ch 2–3 Chen Ch 2 Solve drift problems numerically in Python
Month 3 · Weeks 9–12
Kinetic theory — the Vlasov equation
Derive the BBGKY hierarchy → Vlasov from Liouville. Understand why it is collisionless (Λ ≫ 1). Take the first three velocity moments → recover fluid equations. Identify the closure problem: the pressure equation contains heat flux q, the heat flux equation contains R... This is the central mathematical tension of your research area. End of this block: you should be able to write the Vlasov equation, explain every term, and state what Landau damping is physically.
Kulsrud Ch 3 Boyd & Sanderson Ch 7 Derive moment equations by hand, twice Most skipped: the actual derivation of BBGKY
Month 4 · Weeks 13–16
MHD — ideal and resistive
Derive MHD from two-fluid equations (take mass-weighted average for bulk fluid, electron–ion difference for Ohm's law). Ideal MHD: frozen-in theorem (dΦ_B/dt = 0 following a fluid element), Alfvén waves, MHD equilibria (∇p = J×B). Resistive MHD: finite η breaks frozen-in, enables reconnection. Key number: Lundquist number S = v_A L/η — when S is large (10⁶–10¹²), resistive diffusion is negligible everywhere except thin current sheets.
Kulsrud Ch 4–5 Priest — Solar Magnetohydrodynamics Derive Alfvén wave dispersion from MHD
Month 5–6 · Weeks 17–24
Plasma waves, instabilities, astrophysical contexts
Linear wave theory: substitute e^{i(k·x−ωt)}, derive the dispersion relation ω(k), identify wave modes (Alfvén, fast/slow magnetosonic, whistler, Langmuir, ion acoustic). Landau damping: the imaginary part of ω when the plasma dispersion function Z(ζ) is evaluated correctly. Key instabilities: MRI (drives accretion), firehose (driven by T∥ > T⊥), mirror (T⊥ > T∥), Weibel (anisotropic velocity distribution). End with reading 3–5 classic papers in solar wind or ISM plasma.
Kulsrud Ch 6–7 Stix — Waves in Plasmas Plot dispersion relations in Python plasmapy library
Self-check milestones
Derive all four guiding centre drifts from the equation of motion
Write the Vlasov equation and take its first moment to get the continuity equation
Derive the Alfvén wave dispersion relation ω² = k²_∥ v²_A from linearised MHD
Explain in one sentence why Landau damping occurs without using any equations
State the three conditions under which MHD fails and kinetic theory is required
2
Numerical simulation
Months 5–9 · Overlaps with Phase 1 end
You learn numerics fastest by running and breaking codes. Don't read documentation passively — clone a code, run the test suite, change one parameter, and understand why the output changed.
Month 5 · Python & numerical foundations
Write your own 1D MHD and 1D Vlasov solver
Finite-difference 1D MHD (Lax-Wendroff or MUSCL scheme). Understand: CFL condition (Δt < Δx/v_max), numerical diffusion, ghost cells. Then: 1D electrostatic Vlasov-Poisson on a phase-space grid. This is hard — velocity-space grids need 6th-order finite difference or semi-Lagrangian to avoid filamentation. These toy codes build irreplaceable intuition for what the big codes are actually doing.
Toro — Riemann Solvers NumPy, Matplotlib, yt Break the CFL, observe the instability
Month 6–7 · PIC codes
Run EPOCH or TRISTAN-MP on a shock or reconnection setup
Start with a 1D Weibel instability setup — simplest kinetic problem, fast to run, spectacular velocity-space structure. Then run a 2D reconnection Harris sheet. Focus on: computing velocity-space moments (n, u, P_ij, q_i) from the particle data — this is the exact closure term you would use to train an ML closure. Learn to use HDF5 output and the yt library for analysis.
EPOCH documentation HDF5, yt, h5py Compute q_exact = ∫mv²v_∥ f dv from particles Trap: treating macro-particles as real particles
Month 7–8 · MHD codes
Athena++ — Orszag-Tang vortex and MRI shearing box
Run the Orszag-Tang vortex (classic MHD turbulence test). Then run the MRI shearing box (your future research context). Understand: constrained transport (how Athena++ keeps ∇·B = 0), AMR hierarchy, and how to add custom source terms (this is where you will eventually plug in an ML closure). Compile with problem generator, modify it, re-run — this is the muscle you need for research.
Stone et al. 2008 (Athena++ paper) Athena++, Python analysis scripts Add a custom body force, verify energy conservation
Month 8–9 · HPC basics
MPI parallelism, job submission, profiling
Run Athena++ on 4 MPI ranks, verify identical results to 1 rank. Learn: domain decomposition, ghost cell communication, weak vs. strong scaling. Submit jobs on your cluster with SLURM. Basic profiling: measure time-per-zone-per-step, identify bottleneck subroutines with gprof or Score-P. This is less glamorous than physics but determines whether your simulations finish in days or months.
SLURM, MPI, gprof Run scaling test: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16 ranks
Self-check milestones
Write a 1D MHD solver that passes the Brio-Wu shock tube test
Run a PIC reconnection simulation and plot the out-of-plane B_z (Hall field)
Compute exact heat flux q_∥ from PIC particle data as a velocity integral of f
Run Athena++ MRI shearing box and measure the Maxwell stress ⟨−B_xB_y⟩/4π
3
ML closure — your research frontier
Months 8–14 · The convergence zone
This is where plasma + numerics + ML converge. You are not learning ML from scratch — you are learning which ML tools solve the specific problem of predicting kinetic closure terms from fluid state variables.
Month 8–9 · ML foundations for physics
PyTorch, auto-differentiation, supervised regression
Focus: neural networks as function approximators, not AI hype. Key concepts you actually need — automatic differentiation (why this matters: you can differentiate through the loss function with respect to network weights AND with respect to PDE inputs), batch normalization, weight initialization, and learning rate scheduling. Practical goal: train a 3-layer MLP to predict q_∥ from (β_e, T_aniso, ∇T) using PIC data you already generated.
Goodfellow — Deep Learning (Ch 1–8) PyTorch, JAX, WandB Train MLP on your own PIC data
Month 10–11 · PINNs and neural operators
Physics-informed loss, Fourier neural operator
PINNs: add the MHD PDE residual to your loss function. The network must satisfy both the training data AND the physics equations simultaneously. This enforces conservation laws automatically. FNO (Fourier Neural Operator, Li et al. 2021): learns the solution operator — maps (n, u, T at time t) to closure term q at time t. Resolution-invariant. Read the FNO paper and the plasmapy-ML benchmarks carefully.
Raissi et al. 2019 (PINN paper) Li et al. 2021 (FNO paper) Implement PINN for 1D Burgers equation first Ask LLM to explain PINN loss function term by term
Month 11–12 · Symbolic regression
PySR — discover analytic closure formulas
Instead of a black-box NN, PySR searches for an analytic formula that fits your PIC data. Output: something like q_∥ = −0.73 n T / |k_∥| × (∂T/∂s). This is interpretable, physically meaningful, and can be implemented directly in a Fortran MHD code without any ML library. Miles Cranmer's PySR is the standard tool. Huge advantage for publishing: reviewers trust analytic formulas more than NN weights.
Cranmer et al. 2023 (PySR) PySR, Julia Rediscover Hammett-Perkins from PIC data
Month 12–14 · Deploy closure in MHD
Embed trained NN into Athena++ as a custom module
This is your first publishable result. Steps: export trained NN to LibTorch (C++ inference). Add a custom source term in Athena++ that calls LibTorch at each cell each timestep. Run the MRI shearing box with your NN closure vs. Braginskii closure. Compare effective α_visc, power spectra, and temperature anisotropy profiles. Validate against held-out PIC runs you didn't train on. This workflow is the core of the field.
LibTorch, Athena++ Compare NN-MHD vs. PIC on same initial conditions Validate on out-of-training-distribution cases first
Self-check milestones
Train an MLP to predict parallel heat flux from β_e and temperature gradient with R² > 0.9
Implement a PINN for 1D Vlasov and verify it recovers Landau damping rate
Run PySR on PIC heat flux data and compare discovered formula to Hammett-Perkins
Compile Athena++ with a custom LibTorch closure call and verify it runs stably

Active research habits
Month 6 onward · Permanent practices
Paper reading — daily habit
ArXiv astro-ph.HE + physics.plasm-ph · 20 min/day
Skim titles and abstracts daily. Read one full paper per week deeply. For each paper: (1) what is the closure assumption? (2) what approximation does it break? (3) what would an ML closure fix? Keep a reference manager (Zotero, Paperpile). Start an Obsidian or Notion vault linking papers to concepts.
arXiv, Zotero, Obsidian Weekly 1-page paper summary in your own words
Seminars & group meetings
Attend everything — ask one question per talk
Even if you understand 20% of a seminar, ask one question. It forces you to identify what you understood and what you didn't — more valuable than passive listening. Target groups: your advisor's group, any plasma/astrophysics journal club, and the APS DPP annual meeting (Division of Plasma Physics) as your first conference target.
Pre-read the speaker's most recent paper Write 3 sentences summarising the talk after
Code hygiene — from day 1
Git, tests, reproducibility
Every simulation and analysis script lives in a git repository. Every figure is reproducible from a single command. Every numerical result has a corresponding unit test. This sounds like overhead — it saves you from the nightmare of not being able to reproduce your own results 6 months later. Use pre-commit hooks, GitHub CI, and a consistent directory structure (data/, scripts/, figures/, paper/).
Git, GitHub, pytest, snakemake Trap: raw simulation data with no metadata
Writing — start now
Weekly research log in LaTeX · 500 words minimum
Write every week, even if nothing worked. Describe: what you tried, what broke, what you learned from the failure, and what you plan to test next. This log becomes the first draft of your methods section. Scientific writing is a skill that improves only with practice — starting in year 1 means you are a competent writer by the time you need to submit papers.
LaTeX, Overleaf Figure-first writing: make the plot, then write around it
When you feel shaky on foundations
The right method depends on whether you are gap-filling a specific concept or rebuilding a whole topic. Use these in order of effort — start with the fastest one that might work.
Method 1 · fastest

Targeted derivation

Identify the exact equation you can't derive. Close the book. Try to re-derive it. Every step you get stuck on reveals a precise gap. Then look up only that step. This is 10× more efficient than re-reading a whole chapter.

Method 2 · conversational

LLM Socratic dialogue

Don't ask for an explanation — ask LLM to quiz you. Say: "I'm trying to understand why the canonical momentum p = mv + qA matters. Ask me 5 questions to test my understanding, and tell me when I'm wrong." This is active retrieval, not passive reading.

Method 3 · conceptual

Analogy extraction

Ask LLM: "Explain Landau damping using an analogy from everyday mechanics, then show me how the analogy breaks down." The breakdown is where the real physics lives. Never accept an analogy that doesn't eventually fail — it means it's too vague to be useful.

Method 4 · deepest

Teach it out loud

Record a 5-minute voice explanation of the concept as if teaching a student one year behind you. Play it back. Where you stumble = where you don't actually understand. The Feynman technique works because articulation forces precision.

LLM prompts that actually work for physics review

When you're confused about a derivation

"I'm trying to derive [X]. I got stuck at this step: [paste your working]. What am I missing? Don't give me the full derivation — just tell me what principle I'm not applying correctly at this step."

When a concept feels vague and you can't pin it down

"Give me the most concrete, specific physical example of [concept] — not a general description, a specific number. E.g. for Landau damping: in the solar wind at 1 AU with β_e = 0.5, what is the damping rate of an Alfvén wave at k d_i = 0.5 in units of the wave frequency?"

When you want to test yourself, not be taught

"Give me a problem set of 4 questions on [topic], ordered from straightforward to research-adjacent. Don't give me the answers yet. After I submit my attempts, tell me specifically what is wrong and why — not just the correct answer."

When you need to rebuild a topic fast

"I have 2 hours to rebuild my understanding of [topic] for a meeting tomorrow. What are the 5 key results I must be able to state precisely, and what is the single most important derivation that connects them? Focus on what a working plasma astrophysicist uses daily, not what appears in intro textbooks."
The one rule for using LLMs in grad school
Use LLMs to accelerate understanding of things you are already grappling with — not to bypass the grappling. The grappling is where the learning happens. If you can ask an LLM a question and accept the first answer without pushing back or testing it yourself, you have gained the words but not the physics. The measure of whether you understood something is not "can I recognise this explanation" but "can I produce it, apply it to a new situation, and spot when it is being misused."
